Sonography training - Does practice make perfect?

Ultrasound Training Deliberate Practice

An adapted version of this post was originally provided to first-year medical ultrasound students at QUT to encourage discussion on ways they could accelerate their learning.

Christopher Edwards true
2022-07-26

Ultrasound examinations are unique and rely heavily on the operator’s skill. In contrast, during Computed Tomography (CT) and Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) examinations, image quality is not directly coupled to the operator’s physical dexterity and real-time cognitive processing speed. General Radiography still relies heavily on the ability of the Radiographer to position a patient correctly. However, the skills to precisely set exposure factors depend less on the Radiographer nowadays with the more widespread use of computerised and digital radiography. The distinctive machine-human interface required during an ultrasound exam is necessary and directly influences each exam’s success or failure.

Sonography requires the operator’s integrated skill to manipulate the transducer in various planes to produce a diagnostic image whilst adjusting controls that are not automatically adjusted by the machine. All the while simultaneously interpreting many images per second to determine if any significant pathology is present. The diagnostic ability of an ultrasound scan remains in real-time and at the point of care. Indeed, if a significant pathology is present and not imaged appropriately, it will not be detected in a review by the reporting specialist. This combination of abilities highlights the high level of skills required of a Sonographer. Sonography, in many ways, is like other activities that require a synergy of skills to perform well, such as playing an instrument. A high level of coordination and knowledge of music and how notes and sounds fit well together are required. Or like many sporting activities, which often require superior fitness coupled with specific skills and knowledge of the game. Therefore, the training of a Sonographer takes many years.

Like other health disciples, student sonographers are exposed to various training environments; at times, these are effective and provide ample opportunity to learn and develop. At other times learning can be complex due to external factors.

At this point I proposed some questions and introduced some other information and research in the field of skill acquisition.

…some may be familiar with the terms purposeful or Deliberate Practice (DP). Which has been popularised in several books, some of which are best sellers. The book Outliers: The story of success by Malcolm Gladwell (Gladwell 2008) has several elements, but a vital tenet of the book argues the secret to success is the notion of 10,000 hours of practice. Highlighting that a significant degree of time focused on a task is required to become truly an expert in any field, more so than any innate natural ability or genetic predisposition. Gladwell writes on many levels giving many examples, some personal and others from popular culture such as The Beatles and Bill Gates, and discussing the importance of environment and socio-economic factors being highly correlated to success.

So, a significant amount of time is required, but for many of us, this is predetermined. Such as the days a week we work or are rostered in ultrasound, and some students do more than others. Other authors have stated that there is truth to the 10,000 hours rule, but this is perhaps a little simplistic. Another interesting book is a book by Matthew Syed called Bounce (Syed and Clamp 2010). Matthew Syed is a journalist who writes for the Times in the UK and lectures on many topics about success and high performance; interestingly, he became a world-class table tennis player, achieving fantastic success in this discipline. His book and others like it state that practice is essential, but also practice must be purposeful; otherwise, it is useless. At least sometimes, training must force us out of our comfort zone to “overload” the current systems to cause improvements. Also, he stresses the importance of feedback as necessary for progress, as this helps us correct and fine-tune our abilities.

The above authors have likely drawn much of their influence from the work of Anders Ericsson, a professor of Psychology at Floria State University. He has spent his life researching human performance. Much of his work has investigated the health care field, particularly the skill and expertise of doctors and nurses. His research and others in this field focused on the relative expertise and abilities as doctors progress in their careers. He argued the skills of doctors (at least those working in the clinical and diagnostic areas) could even worsen as time progresses. He explains this as a combination of factors, such as the inability to receive feedback on decisions made. Health care is complicated, and it is often difficult to define which intervention has been effective. In health, there is also the assumption that once you have finished your training and gained the knowledge, you are suitably skilled to perform at a high level. Anders argues that this is far from the truth and discusses the adage “see one, do one, teach one” and how this institutional thinking is damaging.

Health care workers, after a certain point in time, become comfortable with their abilities and, therefore, without knowing it, fail to progress further even if their knowledge advances. Anders argues that the key to the continued improvement of skills will only occur when a few critical components of learning and practice are adhered to. One crucial element is feedback; Anders with Robert Pool, in their book Peak (Ericsson and Pool 2016) explain that some doctors will improve over time but only when they can get direct feedback. He uses the model of surgeons that perform prostatectomies due to the presence of prostate cancer. For the surgery to be effective, the entire prostate must be removed with histologically clear margins when the specimen is sent for analysis. Successful surgery directly impacts the patient’s long-term survival rate, and the doctor will receive the results of their surgery a short time after they have performed it. It has become clear that those surgeons who have performed this operation many more times have much higher patient survival rates. In comparison, those doctors who have performed far fewer surgeries are almost always worse. The more experienced surgeons have practised more, gained feedback, and then adapted their technique to better outcomes. This adaptation, in part, is a product of the learner developing some novel way to solve a complex issue. Anders describes this as “mental representations”, and there is much to this described in his book. However, this youtube video also explains it quite well.

YouTube Video - How to Master Anything: PEAK by Anders Ericsson | Core Message

The core components mentioned in the book (and summary video) are having specific, attainable goals, focusing on the problem at hand, gaining feedback and practising at least part of the time right at the limit of our abilities. Added to this is an effective coach to ensure the best chance to improve. Importantly, improvement occurs much quicker when these elements are present than simply spending time “just doing.”

So how does any of this apply to Sonography? At this point, I asked the students to consider some of the above topics and provide insights from their own lived experiences.

To get the ball rolling, I stated feedback is essential. For many students, feedback, particularly on areas of technique, is likely regularly provided early in training. However, this type of direct feedback becomes less common as the basic skills and knowledge of probe manipulation and scan technique are understood. So, are there other ways students can continue to gather feedback in busy clinical environments?

Anders also points out how the health care industry is excellent at promoting and providing opportunities for continuing professional development (CPD). There are endless opportunities to attend seminars and lectures on various discipline-specific areas. Still, he argues most of these are useless and don’t improve the skills of those that attend. Remember, knowledge does not equate to a new skill. On the contrary, workshops are immensely effective, where a student can gain hands-on training whilst being instructed by an expert in a particular area. Food for thought, perhaps we should preference the available hands-on workshops at conferences rather than bothering with all the lectures? Something I think the Sonography profession already does well and provides a good balance.

What about mental representations? Are there ways students can develop these? Anders offers some potential insights on this, and the example in the book (and video above), about his students who was tasked with remembering number sequences, is a good one. He had an accomplished student Steve explain to another student Dario his technique of remembering the numbers like a tree branch, which subsequently allowed Dario to improve rapidly and even surpass Steve’s abilities to recall the numbers. Anders also uses the example of training schemes with doctors where during complex operations, he had expert doctors explain what they were thinking during the procedure by asking specific questions. This process teased out those mental representations that may be subconscious for many experts but made a massive difference in student learning. For other ideas related to ultrasound, my colleagues and I wrote an article on Deliberate practice in Sonography a few years ago (Edwards, Chamunyonga, and Clarke 2018).

In summary, the task was for students to reflect on their own experiences and consider ways they could take greater responsibility to enhance their learning outcomes. The ideas and background in Deliberate Practice provided a valuable springboard and produced a range of good ideas and insights from the student, which they generously shared with their peers. Please feel free to use and adapt for your students in ultrasound or other professions.
